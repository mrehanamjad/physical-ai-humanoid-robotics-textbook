This document records the key decisions made during the planning phase for Chapter 1.

## Decision: Conceptual Depth vs. Mathematical Rigor

-   **Decision**: Prioritize conceptual depth and intuitive understanding over mathematical rigor.
-   **Rationale**: As an introductory chapter, the primary goal is to build a strong mental framework for students from diverse backgrounds (CS and robotics). Introducing heavy mathematical formalism at this stage would be a barrier to entry and is not essential for grasping the core concepts of Physical AI.
-   **Alternatives considered**:
    -   A mathematically rigorous approach was rejected to ensure accessibility.
    -   A hybrid model with mathematical appendices was considered but deferred to later chapters to maintain a clear narrative flow.

## Decision: Order of Historical Context vs. Modern Physical AI Framing

-   **Decision**: Begin with a modern framing of Physical AI to immediately capture student interest, then provide historical context to ground the concepts.
-   **Rationale**: Starting with modern, relevant examples (e.g., self-driving cars, humanoid robots) makes the subject more engaging. Historical context is important but serves better to enrich the modern concepts rather than introduce them.
-   **Alternatives considered**:
    -   A purely chronological approach was rejected as potentially being too dry for an introduction.

## Decision: Diagram Usage vs. Text Explanations

-   **Decision**: Employ a balanced approach, where every key concept (like the perception-action loop) is supported by a clear, well-annotated diagram. Text will be used to elaborate on the diagrams, not replace them.
-   **Rationale**: Complex systems and loops are more easily understood visually. Diagrams provide an intuitive entry point, which can then be fleshed out with concise text.
-   **Alternatives considered**:
    -   A text-heavy approach was rejected as being too dense for an introductory chapter.
    -   A primarily visual approach was rejected as it would lack the necessary detail and nuance.

## Decision: Use of Examples vs. Theory-First Explanation

-   **Decision**: Lead with simple, relatable examples to introduce a concept, then follow up with the formal theory or definition.
-   **Rationale**: This "example-first" approach makes abstract concepts more concrete and easier to digest. It follows a natural learning progression from the specific to the general.
-   **Alternatives considered**:
    -   A "theory-first" approach was rejected as it can be intimidating and less effective for building intuition.
